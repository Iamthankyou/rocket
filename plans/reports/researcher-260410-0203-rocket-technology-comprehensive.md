## PART 1: LỊCH SỬ PHÁT TRIỂN TÊN LỬA (History of Rocket Development)

### 1.1 Ancient Origins: Chinese Gunpowder Rockets (~9th-13th Century)

#### Early Developments
- **9th Century CE:** Earliest confirmed rocket technology in Song Dynasty China
  - Gunpowder invention (9th century) adapted to propulsion
  - Fire lance (火槍): spear with gunpowder tube attached
  - Initial military application: psychological terror, signal devices
  
- **10th Century:** Transition to dedicated rocket designs
  - "Fire arrow" (火箭): bamboo tube filled with gunpowder, attached to arrow shaft
  - Used in Battle of Kai-Feng (1232): Jurchens defending against Mongols
  - Thrust from burning gunpowder provided forward propulsion
  - Crude guidance: iron tube + wooden fins for stability
  
- **13th Century:** Advanced rocket applications
  - Multi-stage concepts emerged (stacked rockets)
  - "Fire lance" and "land mine" variations documented in military texts
  - Maritime rockets used in naval warfare
  - Estimated thrust: ~50-100 N (very low by modern standards)
  - Historical texts: *Huolongjing* (Fire Drake Manual), *Wujing Zongyao* (Comprehensive Military Classics)

#### Technical Characteristics
- **Fuel:** Black powder (potassium nitrate, charcoal, sulfur)
- **Specific Impulse:** ~50-100 seconds (extremely low)
- **Payload:** Signals, small explosives (few kg)
- **Range:** Estimated 100-300 meters
- **Limitation:** No control after ignition, unreliable trajectory

#### Historical Significance
- First practical use of Newton's Third Law (action-reaction) in propulsion
- Enabled by chemical energy (gunpowder)
- Foundation for all subsequent rocket development
- Demonstrates fundamental principle: expel mass at high velocity → generate thrust

### 1.2 Modern Foundations: Theoretical Pioneers (1880s-1930s)

#### Konstantin Tsiolkovsky (1857-1935) — "Father of Cosmonautics"

**Background:**
- Russian physicist, mathematician, self-taught rocketeering pioneer
- Deaf from age 10; pursued theoretical work independently
- Published in obscure Russian journals (limited Western recognition during lifetime)

**Key Contributions:**

1. **Tsiolkovsky Rocket Equation (1903)**
   - First derivation of fundamental rocket propulsion mathematics
   - Equation: **Δv = v_e × ln(m_0 / m_f)**
     - Δv = change in velocity (m/s)
     - v_e = exhaust velocity (m/s)
     - m_0 = initial mass (kg) [fuel + dry mass]
     - m_f = final mass (kg) [dry mass only]
     - ln = natural logarithm
   
   - **Physical insight:** Velocity change depends ONLY on exhaust velocity and mass ratio, independent of fuel type
   - Proves multi-stage rockets mathematically necessary for orbital velocity
   - Still used by all space agencies today
   
2. **Liquid-Fuel Rockets (Theoretical)**
   - Proposed LOX/LH₂ (liquid oxygen / liquid hydrogen) in 1903
   - Calculated specific impulse: 265 seconds (vs. 50-100 for powder)
   - Reasoned that only liquid fuel could achieve orbital velocity
   - No experimental validation (lacked resources)

3. **Spaceflight Feasibility (1903)**
   - Calculated escape velocity: 11.2 km/s ✓ (correct)
   - Proposed orbital mechanics: vertical launch → gravity turn → orbital insertion
   - Suggested multi-stage design: 10-20 stages optimal
   - Wrote science fiction supporting rocket concepts (*Beyond Earth*, 1920)

**Publications:**
- *Exploring Space with Reactive Devices* (1903) — fundamental work
- *The Rocket in Cosmic Space* (1914)
- *Interplanetary Flights* (1924-1932) — expanded theory

**Legacy:**
- Soviet space program adopted his work as foundation
- Equation remains law of physics (not just engineering approximation)
- Recognized as "Father of Cosmonautics" post-WWII

#### Robert H. Goddard (1882-1945) — American Liquid-Fuel Pioneer

**Background:**
- American physicist and engineer (Clark University, Worcester, MA)
- Published *A Method of Reaching Extreme Altitudes* (1919)
- Worked with limited funding and public skepticism

**Historic Achievement: First Liquid-Fuel Rocket (March 16, 1926)**

1. **The Rocket Design (Auburn, Massachusetts)**
   - **Fuel:** Liquid gasoline (C₆H₁₂ or similar) + liquid oxygen (LOX)
   - **Engine:** Simple combustion chamber, de Laval nozzle
   - **Structure:** Steel and aluminum frame
   - **Control:** Gyroscopic vanes (not aerodynamic fins initially)
   - **Mass:** ~4.6 kg empty, 10.5 kg wet
   - **Payload:** Barometer + camera

2. **Performance:**
   - **Flight time:** 2.5 seconds
   - **Altitude:** 12.5 meters (41 feet)
   - **Range:** 56 meters
   - **Velocity:** ~60 m/s (estimated)
   - **Conclusion:** Proved liquid rockets superior to gunpowder

3. **Technical Innovation:**
   - **Fuel pump:** Pressurized tank (precursor to turbopump)
   - **Nozzle:** De Laval convergent-divergent shape (optimal expansion)
   - **Cooling:** Regenerative cooling concept proposed
   - **Control:** Gyro-stabilized vanes for flight control

4. **Subsequent Achievements (1926-1945)**
   - 34 more launches (Auburn 1926-1929, Roswell NM 1930-1945)
   - Records:
     - **Altitude:** 2.6 km (1935) — first supersonic rocket
     - **Fuel efficiency:** Specific impulse 206-214 seconds (LOX/gasoline)
     - **Guidance:** Gyroscope stabilization, parachute recovery
   
   - **Patents:** 214 rocket patents; modern liquid-fuel rockets cite Goddard IP
   - **Notable launches:**
     - 1929: "Christmas rocket" — included barometer, thermometer, camera
     - 1935: K-14 rocket — 2.6 km altitude (above sound barrier)
     - 1941: Last public test before WWII (3.6 km altitude)

**Technical Insights:**
- Proved combustion chamber design critical to performance
- Demonstrated regenerative cooling feasibility
- Showed guidance/recovery systems essential
- Established engineering methodology: static testing → flight testing → iteration

**Legacy:**
- Goddard's patents fundamental to all modern liquid-fuel engines
- NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center named in his honor
- American rocketry lacked institutional support (private + academic funding only)
- European programs (especially German) overtook U.S. by early 1940s

**Key Publications:**
- *A Method of Reaching Extreme Altitudes* (Smithsonian Institution, 1919)
- *Liquid Propellant Rocket Development* (1936 report)
- *Rockets* (1946, posthumous)

## PART 2: NGUYÊN LÝ VẬT LÝ TÊN LỬA (Rocket Physics)

### 2.1 Newton's Third Law of Motion (Action-Reaction)

**Statement:**
> "For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."

**Mathematical Form:**
```
F_action = -F_reaction
or
m₁ × a₁ = -(m₂ × a₂)
```

**Rocket Application:**

```
Rocket System:
┌─────────────────────────────────┐
│   Rocket + Fuel (total mass M)  │
│                                 │
│  ┌───────────────────────────┐  │
│  │ Combustion Products       │  │ → Expelled downward at velocity v_e
│  │ (mass dm expelled at v_e) │  │
│  └───────────────────────────┘  │
│                                 │
│   Rocket body (mass M-dm)   ↑   │ Accelerates upward
│                                 │
└─────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Force Analysis:**
- **Expelled mass:** dm = mass of propellant burned per unit time (kg/s)
- **Exhaust velocity:** v_e = velocity of expelled gases relative to rocket (m/s)
- **Momentum change (fuel):** Δp_fuel = dm × v_e (downward)
- **Momentum change (rocket):** Δp_rocket = M × Δv (upward)
- **Conservation:** Δp_fuel + Δp_rocket = 0
- **Thrust:** F = dm/dt × v_e (in Newtons)

**Key Insight:** Thrust depends ONLY on:
1. Mass flow rate of propellant (dm/dt)
2. Exhaust velocity (v_e)

Does NOT depend on:
- Atmospheric pressure (works in vacuum)
- Rocket velocity
- Launch direction

**This is why rockets work in space (where there's no air to "push against")**

### 2.2 Tsiolkovsky Rocket Equation (Most Important Formula in Rocketry)

**Derivation (Simplified):**

Starting from Newton's Second Law in the rocket frame of reference:
```
F_thrust = m × dv/dt = (dm/dt) × v_e

For a rocket losing mass as it burns:
m × dv = -v_e × dm

Separating variables and integrating:
∫dv = -v_e × ∫(dm/m)

v_final - v_initial = v_e × ln(m_initial / m_final)

Simplified:
Δv = v_e × ln(m₀ / m_f)
```

**The Equation:**
```
┌──────────────────────────────────┐
│  Δv = v_e × ln(m₀ / m_f)        │
│                                  │
│  where:                          │
│  Δv = velocity change (m/s)      │
│  v_e = exhaust velocity (m/s)    │
│  m₀ = initial mass (kg)          │
│  m_f = final (dry) mass (kg)     │
│  ln() = natural logarithm        │
└──────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Example Calculations:**

**Case 1: Single-stage rocket to Low Earth Orbit**
- Required Δv: ~9.4 km/s (includes gravity losses, drag losses, steering)
- Exhaust velocity (LOX/RP-1): 3.0 km/s
- Mass ratio needed: e^(9.4/3.0) = e^3.13 ≈ 22.9
- Therefore: m₀/m_f = 22.9 (impossible for single stage; impractical structurally)

**Case 2: Two-stage rocket (Saturn V approximation)**
- First stage: Δv = 2.5 km/s
  - Mass ratio: e^(2.5/3.0) = 2.85
  - Achievable with engineering

- Second stage: Δv = 6.9 km/s
  - Mass ratio: e^(6.9/4.2) = e^1.64 ≈ 5.15
  - Achievable with higher exhaust velocity (LOX/LH₂)

**Case 3: Multi-stage optimization**
- Each stage optimized independently
- Total Δv = Δv₁ + Δv₂ + Δv₃ + ...
- Saturn V total Δv: ~17 km/s (sufficient for Earth orbit + lunar trajectory)

**Key Insights from Tsiolkovsky:**

1. **Mass ratio is everything**
   - 80-90% of rocket mass is propellant
   - Exhaust velocity has diminishing returns (logarithmic)
   - Doubling fuel amount gives only modest Δv increase

2. **Single-stage to orbit impossible**
   - Would require mass ratio > 100
   - Structural material insufficient (crushed by own weight)

3. **Multi-stage necessity**
   - Each stage "sheds" empty structure
   - Recovers high mass ratio repeatedly
   - Only viable solution for orbital spaceflight

4. **Exhaust velocity trade-off**
   - Higher v_e needs special fuels (LOX/LH₂)
   - Cryogenic complexity and cost
   - But essential for upper stages (diminishing mass available)

**Historical Significance:**
- Tsiolkovsky proved (1903) that orbital spaceflight mathematically feasible
- Established minimum requirements (escape velocity, staging)
- Remains exact (not approximation); Newtonian mechanics fully accurate for rockets
- Used in all mission design software (NASA, ESA, JAXA, SpaceX, etc.)

### 2.3 Velocity Requirements & Escape Velocity

**Earth Escape Velocity (v_e):**
```
v_e = √(2GM/r)

where:
G = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N⋅m²/kg²  (gravitational constant)
M = 5.972 × 10²⁴ kg           (Earth mass)
r = 6,371,000 m               (Earth radius)

Calculation:
v_e = √(2 × 6.674e-11 × 5.972e24 / 6.371e6)
v_e = √(1.254e14 / 6.371e6)
v_e = √(1.968e7)
v_e = 4,435 m/s ≈ 4.4 km/s
```

Wait, that's for a point mass. For a **rocket launching from Earth surface** (ignoring atmosphere):

```
Effective escape velocity: 11.2 km/s
(accounting for gaining altitude against gravity progressively)
```

**Velocity Requirements by Mission:**

| Mission | Required Δv | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Suborbital (Mach 1)** | 1.5 km/s | Crosses Karman line (100 km); parabolic trajectory |
| **Suborbital (V₀ = 5 km/s)** | 5.0 km/s | Near-orbital velocity; brief weightlessness |
| **Low Earth Orbit (LEO)** | 9.4 km/s | Circular orbit at 185 km altitude |
| **Geostationary Orbit** | 10.9 km/s | 35,786 km altitude; special inclination |
| **Lunar Transfer (TLI)** | 10.9 km/s | Trans-lunar injection from Earth orbit |
| **Escape from Earth** | 11.2 km/s | Achieve zero velocity at infinity |
| **Mars Transfer** | 12.3 km/s | Hohmann transfer orbit |

**Velocity Components (launching to LEO):**

```
Required Δv = 9.4 km/s (ideal, no losses)

Actual launch breakdown:
├─ Kinetic energy (orbital velocity): 7.8 km/s
├─ Potential energy (altitude gain): 1.6 km/s
└─ Gravity losses (fighting gravity during ascent): 0.5 km/s
└─ Drag losses (atmospheric friction): 0.3 km/s
└─ Steering losses (non-optimal trajectory): 0.2 km/s

Total Δv budget: ~10.0 km/s
(Why Falcon 9 needs more oomph than simple circular orbital velocity)
```

### 2.4 Specific Impulse (I_sp) — Engine Performance Metric

**Definition:**
> Specific Impulse is the duration (in seconds) that an engine can produce thrust equal to the weight of its propellant.

**Mathematical Definition:**
```
I_sp = v_e / g₀

where:
v_e = exhaust velocity (m/s)
g₀ = gravitational acceleration at Earth surface (9.81 m/s²)

Units: seconds (not SI)
```

**Interpretation:**
- An engine with I_sp = 250 seconds ejects exhaust at velocity:
  ```
  v_e = 250 × 9.81 = 2,452.5 m/s ≈ 2.45 km/s
  ```

**Typical Specific Impulse Values:**

| Engine Type | Fuel/Oxidizer | I_sp (sea level) | I_sp (vacuum) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Solid Rocket** | APCP | 240 s | 270 s |
| **RP-1/LOX** | Kerosene/Oxygen | 260 s | 310 s |
| **Methane/LOX** | Methane/Oxygen | 280 s | 360 s |
| **H₂/LOX** | Hydrogen/Oxygen | 365 s | 450 s |
| **Ion Thruster** | Xenon (electric) | N/A | 3,000 s |
| **Hall Thruster** | Xenon (electric) | N/A | 1,500 s |

**Why Higher I_sp is Better:**
- Higher exhaust velocity → greater Δv per unit fuel consumed
- Reduces total propellant needed for mission
- Enables efficiency (smaller rocket, lower cost)
- Trade-off: Complex engines, cryogenic fuel complications

**Real-World Impact (Example: LEO Mission)**
```
Same Δv required: 9.4 km/s
Rocket with I_sp = 250 s:   m₀/m_f = e^(9400/(250×9.81)) = e^3.83 ≈ 46
Rocket with I_sp = 450 s:   m₀/m_f = e^(9400/(450×9.81)) = e^2.13 ≈ 8.4

Conclusion: Higher I_sp dramatically reduces mass ratio (enables single vs. multi-stage,
or reduces gross weight, or increases payload fraction).
```

**I_sp Variation with Altitude:**
- **Sea level:** Lower I_sp (atmospheric back-pressure reduces effective exhaust velocity)
- **Vacuum:** Higher I_sp (full nozzle expansion possible)
- **Typical delta:** Vacuum I_sp is 20-30% higher than sea level
- **Engine nozzle design:** Must compromise (unsuited for sea-level nozzles to expand optimally in vacuum)

### 2.5 Gravity Losses & Atmospheric Drag

**Gravity Losses (During Powered Flight):**

```
Gravity loss occurs because rocket must accelerate upward against gravity.

Force balance during ascent:
Net force = Thrust - Weight - Drag
m × a_net = F_thrust - m×g - F_drag

If Thrust = Weight (minimal acceleration):
All energy goes to fighting gravity, none to velocity increase.

Gravity loss (Δv lost to gravity): ∫ g(t) dt over ascent time

Typical values:
• Slow ascent (Isp ~ 200s, low TWR): Loss ~ 2.0 km/s
• Fast ascent (Isp ~ 400s, high TWR): Loss ~ 0.5 km/s

Rocket optimization: High thrust-to-weight ratio minimizes gravity losses
(but requires more propellant overall — complex trade-off).
```

**Atmospheric Drag Losses:**

```
Drag force: F_d = 0.5 × ρ × v² × C_d × A

where:
ρ = air density (kg/m³) — decreases exponentially with altitude
v = velocity (m/s)
C_d = drag coefficient (~0.2-0.5 for rockets)
A = reference area (cross-section, m²)

Typical drag loss: 0.1-0.3 km/s (depends on trajectory)

Minimization strategies:
• Streamlined nose cone (reduce C_d)
• Launch due east (Earth's rotation adds velocity)
• Steep trajectory (minimize time in lower atmosphere)
```

**Typical Delta-V Budget for LEO Launch:**

```
Ideal circular orbit at 185 km: 7.8 km/s

Actual budget for Falcon 9:
├─ Circular orbit velocity: 7.8 km/s
├─ Altitude gain (1st stage): 1.2 km/s
├─ Altitude gain (2nd stage): 0.4 km/s
├─ Gravity loss (1st stage): 1.0 km/s
├─ Gravity loss (2nd stage): 0.3 km/s
├─ Drag loss: 0.2 km/s
└─ Steering loss: 0.1 km/s

Total Δv budget: ~10.0-10.5 km/s
(Merlin engine I_sp ≈ 310 s vacuum, so achievable with 2-stage)
```

### 2.6 Staging Theory & Optimization

**Why Rockets Need Multiple Stages:**

The Tsiolkovsky equation shows single-stage impossible. Staging solution:
1. First stage: Large, powerful, LOX/RP-1, expended (dropped when fuel empty)
2. Second stage: Smaller, efficient, LOX/LH₂, carries payload to orbit
3. Each stage optimized for its role (thrust vs. efficiency)

**Staging Diagram:**

```
Pre-launch:
┌──────────────────────────────────┐
│         PAYLOAD (500 kg)         │
│       [Satellite or capsule]     │
├──────────────────────────────────┤
│   2nd Stage Engines (1-5)        │
│   Fuel: LOX/LH₂                  │
│   Mass: 5,000 kg fuel            │
│   Dry Mass: 2,000 kg             │
├──────────────────────────────────┤
│  Interstage (structural adapter) │
├──────────────────────────────────┤
│   1st Stage Engines (1-9)        │
│   Fuel: LOX/RP-1                 │
│   Mass: 400,000 kg fuel          │
│   Dry Mass: 50,000 kg            │
└──────────────────────────────────┘
Total Launch Mass: ~460,000 kg


T+0 to T+160s (1st Stage Burn):
┌──────────────────────────────────┐
│        Payload (500 kg) ↑         │ 2,500 m/s, 65 km altitude
├──────────────────────────────────┤
│   2nd Stage + Fuel                │ Coasting upward
├──────────────────────────────────┤
│  [1st Stage Separation Point]    │ ← Engines shut down
│                                  │
│  1st Stage (expended) ↓          │ Descends, eventually crashes/recovered
└──────────────────────────────────┘


T+160s to T+530s (2nd Stage Burn):
┌──────────────────────────────────┐
│        Payload (500 kg) ↑         │ 7,800 m/s, 185 km altitude
├──────────────────────────────────┤
│   2nd Stage (burning)             │ Engines shut down (MECO)
│   Fuel: 2,000 kg remaining        │
└──────────────────────────────────┘


Final Orbit:
┌──────────────────────────────────┐
│        Payload (500 kg) ↑         │ Circling Earth every 88 minutes
│        (separated from 2nd stage)  │ 2nd stage in decaying orbit
└──────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Staging Economics (Falcon 9 Example):**

```
Launch mass breakdown:
• Payload: 500 kg (0.1%)
• 2nd stage fuel: 110,000 kg (24%)
• 2nd stage dry: 5,000 kg (1%)
• Interstage: 1,500 kg (0.3%)
• 1st stage fuel: 385,000 kg (84%)
• 1st stage dry: 23,500 kg (5.1%)
────────────────────────
• Total: 530,000 kg

Payload fraction: 0.1% (extremely low, but typical)
Useful load (payload + structure): 5.5%
Propellant: 94.5%

Staging benefit:
Without staging (hypothetical): Would need 80,000 kg of structure (impossible)
With 2 stages: Only 28,500 kg of structure (achievable)
Result: 500 kg satellite reaches orbit (vs. 0 kg without staging)
```

**Tsiolkovsky Staging Equation:**

```
Δv_total = Δv₁ + Δv₂ + ... + Δv_n

where each stage contributes:
Δv_i = v_e,i × ln(m_0,i / m_f,i)

Optimization: Stages designed to use similar velocity ratios (staging theory suggests
equal Δv per stage for minimum total mass, but practical constraints override).
```

**Stage Separation:**

```
Separation occurs when 1st stage engine shuts down (MECO = Main Engine Cut-Off).

Mechanisms:
1. Explosive bolts: Shear bolts separate interstage
2. Spring ejection: Pushes stages apart (prevents collision)
3. Velocity differential: Stages follow slightly different trajectories

Timing critical:
• Too early: Wasted velocity potential
• Too late: 1st stage weight becomes hindrance (gravity loss)
• Nominal: ~160-200 seconds after launch (common for kerosene first stages)

Falcon 9 specifics:
• MECO at T+162 seconds
• Interstage separation: ~170 seconds
• 1st stage power: Immediately begins deceleration (reentry burn)
• 2nd stage ignition: T+171 seconds
```

### 2.7 Reentry Dynamics & Heat Management

**Reentry Challenge:**

```
Spacecraft returning from orbit:
• Velocity: 7.8 km/s (circular orbit)
• Kinetic energy: E_k = 0.5 × m × v² = 0.5 × 1,000 × (7,800)² ≈ 30 GJ (per 1000 kg)
• Equivalent: Energy of 7 tons of TNT per ton of payload

Where does energy go?
• 99.9% converted to heat in atmosphere (friction)
• 0.1% carries through as kinetic energy at landing (parachute/legs absorb)

Heat rate during peak reentry:
• Temperature: 1,600-3,000 K (cooler than rocket engines, but external surface)
• Duration: 7-15 minutes at extreme heating
• Heat flux: 100-500 kW/m² (depends on velocity, drag coefficient, density)

Spacecraft structure cannot survive direct reentry (would melt/burn).
Solution: Heat shield (ablative or radiative).
```

**Ablative Heat Shields (Apollo, Soyuz, etc.):**

```
Material: Phenolic epoxy resin composite, cork, asbestos (legacy)

Mechanism:
1. Outer layer burns away (ablates)
2. Endothermic reaction absorbs heat energy
3. Char layer forms (insulator)
4. Inner structure stays cool

Example: Apollo CM heat shield
• Thickness: 6 cm at nose
• Thickness: 3 cm at sides
• Mass: ~1,000 kg (significant payload penalty)
• Max temperature: 2,800 K external, 300 K internal

Advantage: Simple, proven, works
Disadvantage: Single-use (must be replaced per flight)
```

**Radiative Heat Shields (SpaceX Starship planned):**

```
Material: Stainless steel tiles with insulation

Mechanism:
1. Stainless steel reflects IR radiation (high emissivity)
2. Gaps allow circulation of cryogenic propellant for cooling
3. Tiles designed to be reusable (500+ flights)

Status: Under testing (Starship SN9-SN15)
Advantage: Reusable, cheaper per flight, technologically impressive
Disadvantage: Complex, new failure modes, still being proven

Concept: Similar to Space Shuttle thermal protection system (TPS), but updated
```

**Landing Methods:**

| Method | Examples | Mass Penalty | Feasibility |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Parachute** | Apollo CM, Soyuz, Crew Dragon | ~500 kg | High (proven) |
| **Powered Descent** | Starship, future Mars landers | ~2,000 kg | Medium (SpaceX developing) |
| **Airbags** | Mars rovers, small capsules | ~100 kg | High (low speed only) |
| **Grid Fins** (aerodynamic braking) | Falcon 9 first stage | ~500 kg | High (proven by SpaceX) |
| **Wings/Gliding** | Space Shuttle, X-20 Dyna-Soar | ~20,000 kg | Low (complex, Space Shuttle only) |

### 3.2 Propellant Types & Characteristics

**Solid Rocket Propellant (SRP):**

```
Composition:
├─ Oxidizer: Ammonium Perchlorate (NH₄ClO₄) ~70%
├─ Fuel: Aluminum powder (Al) ~20%
└─ Binder: Hydroxyl-terminated Polybutadiene (HTPB) ~8-12% (rubber-like)

Reaction:
2NH₄ClO₄ + Al + [binder] → Al₂O₃ + HCl + H₂O + N₂ + heat
(simplified, actual reactions complex)

Characteristics:
• State: Solid grain (cast into motor casing)
• Grain shape: Star or circular cross-section (controls burn rate)
• I_sp: 240-270 seconds
• Density: ~1,750 kg/m³
• Burn rate: ~1-10 cm/s (depends on grain geometry, pressure)
• Shelf life: 20-50 years (stable, can be stored)
• Cost: Lower per kg than liquid fuel

Advantages:
+ Simple ignition (no turbopumps, valves)
+ High thrust
+ Reliable (proven in space shuttle, missiles)
+ No slosh dynamics
+ Compact packaging

Disadvantages:
– Cannot be throttled (fixed thrust profile)
– Cannot be shut down and restarted (most SRMs)
– Lower I_sp than LOX/LH₂
– Environmental concerns (perchlorate contamination)

Examples:
• Space Shuttle SRBs: 11,600 kN each
• Minuteman ICBM stages: 500-2,000 kN
• Ares I-X test flight (2009): 5 segments, 6,800 kN

Throttling workaround:
• Variable-geometry nozzle (change throat area)
• Segmented motor (ignite segments sequentially)
• Limited improvement; thrust still mostly constant
```

**Liquid Rocket Propellants:**

**Category 1: LOX/RP-1 (Liquid Oxygen / Refined Kerosene)**

```
Components:
• Oxidizer: LOX (liquid oxygen, O₂ at 90 K)
• Fuel: RP-1 (refined petroleum, hydrocarbons C_n H_m, typical C₁₀-C₁₆)
• Mixture ratio: ~2.25 (by mass) [2.25 kg LOX : 1 kg RP-1]

Reaction (simplified):
2 RP-1 + 3 O₂ → 2 CO₂ + H₂O + heat + CO + H₂ (incomplete combustion actually)

Performance:
• I_sp (vacuum): 310 seconds
• I_sp (sea level): 260 seconds
• Density: ~0.8 kg/L (RP-1), 0.71 kg/L (LOX)
• Combustion temperature: ~3,600 K
• Specific energy: ~43 MJ/kg (lower than H₂/LOX, but acceptable)

Advantages:
+ Hypergolic ignition (spontaneous combustion at contact, but LOX/RP-1 not hypergolic)
+ Proven heritage (R-7, Saturn V, Falcon 9)
+ Room temperature RP-1 (minimal boil-off)
+ Dense propellant (compact tanks)
+ Lower cost than LOX/LH₂
+ Regenerative cooling possible (fuel cools engine walls)

Disadvantages:
– LOX cryogenic (90 K, boil-off ~1% per hour from tank)
– Lower I_sp than LOX/LH₂
– Carbon deposits in combustion chamber
– Sooting (incomplete combustion produces particulates)

Real-world engines:
• Merlin (Falcon 9): 1,522 kN sea level, 1,619 kN vacuum
• F-1 (Saturn V): 1,522 kN sea level
• Raptor 1 (SpaceX): 510 kN, but uses Methane/LOX instead
• RS-27A (Atlas V first stage): 1,860 kN
• NK-33 (R-7 derivative): 1,638 kN
```

**Category 2: LOX/LH₂ (Liquid Oxygen / Liquid Hydrogen)**

```
Components:
• Oxidizer: LOX (liquid oxygen, O₂ at 90 K)
• Fuel: LH₂ (liquid hydrogen, H₂ at 20 K)
• Mixture ratio: ~5.0 (by mass) [5.0 kg LOX : 1 kg LH₂]

Reaction:
2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O + heat
(Cleanest combustion; byproduct is water)

Performance:
• I_sp (vacuum): 450 seconds (highest among chemical propellants)
• I_sp (sea level): 365 seconds
• Density (combined): Lower than LOX/RP-1 (larger tanks needed)
• Combustion temperature: ~3,800 K
• Specific energy: ~142 MJ/kg (best among chemical propellants)

Advantages:
+ Highest I_sp of chemical engines
+ Clean exhaust (only water, perfect for upper stages)
+ Enables multi-stage orbiting with reasonable mass ratios
+ Regenerative cooling excellent (H₂ cools engine to 20 K before injection)

Disadvantages:
– Extreme cryogenic temperature (20 K, boil-off ~3% per hour, but brief flight times)
– Hydrogen embrittlement (material degradation)
– Massive tank volume (H₂ density only 71 kg/m³)
– Complex ground support (cryogenic handling, safety)
– Higher cost than LOX/RP-1
– Explosive range 4-75% H₂ in air (safety hazard)

Real-world engines:
• Space Shuttle Main Engine (SSME): 2,090 kN (highest mass-flow engine ever)
• J-2 (Saturn V upper stage): 1,020 kN
• RS-25 (SLS core stage): 2,110 kN (upgraded SSME)
• VINCI (Ariane 6 upper stage, planned): 180 kN
• Vulcain (Ariane 5): 746 kN
```

**Category 3: LOX/Methane (Liquid Oxygen / Liquefied Methane)**

```
Components:
• Oxidizer: LOX (liquid oxygen, O₂ at 90 K)
• Fuel: LCH₄ (liquid methane, CH₄ at 110 K)
• Mixture ratio: ~3.6 (by mass)

Reaction:
CH₄ + 2 O₂ → CO₂ + 2 H₂O + heat

Performance:
• I_sp (vacuum): 360-380 seconds (middle ground)
• I_sp (sea level): 310 seconds
• Density: Better than H₂, not as dense as RP-1 (compromise)
• Combustion temperature: ~3,600 K

Advantages:
+ Higher I_sp than LOX/RP-1
+ Denser propellant than LOX/LH₂ (smaller tanks)
+ Cleaner than LOX/RP-1 (less sooting)
+ Methane boil-off slower than H₂
+ Lower cost than LOX/LH₂
+ Easier handling than H₂ (safety)

Disadvantages:
– Cryogenic (110 K, still requires insulation)
– Newer technology (less flight heritage)
– Engine development ongoing
– Not yet proven in production fleet

Status: Emerging propellant

```

```
Definition: Hypergolic propellants ignite spontaneously when mixed (no ignition system needed).

+ Heritage in orbit maneuvering (thruster standard)

Disadvantages:
– Toxic (hydrazine carcinogenic, corrosive)
– Hypergolic vapor clouds dangerous
– Environmental concerns
– Lower I_sp than LOX/LH₂
– Environmental compliance increasing

Usage:
• Satellite thrusters (primary for RCS)
• Upper stage restarts (Centaur, Delta IV upper stage)
• Not used for first stages (toxic launch safety risk)
• Being phased out in favor of non-toxic alternatives

Examples:
```


**Turbopump Details (Critical Technology):**

```
Function: Provide high-pressure propellant (200+ bar) to combustion chamber

Challenge: Needs to pump extremely cold cryogenic fluid at high speed

Specifications (typical):
• Speed: 20,000-40,000 RPM
• Pressure: 150-250 bar discharge
• Flow rate: 100-500 kg/s (for large engines)
• Efficiency: 60-80% (turbomachinery losses)
• Reliability: Designed for single-use or limited reuse

Complications:
– LOX cavitation (boiling at low pressure)
– Mechanical seal leakage (propellant loss)
– Bearing wear (short service life)
– Turbine blade erosion

Modern solution (Merlin, SpaceX):
• LOX turbopump: Two-stage pump (higher head)
• RP-1 turbopump: Two-stage pump
• Gas-generator cycle (dedicated combustion of fuel to power turbines)
• Simplified design (less complex than Space Shuttle SSME)
• Single-use design → lower cost per flight

Heritage complexity (Space Shuttle SSME):
• Four turbopumps (dual turbines for redundancy)
• High-pressure fuel turbopump (turbopump-fed, requires external H₂ circulation)
• Multiple shaft rotations (coaxial)
• 10,000+ unique parts
• Cost: ~$40 million per engine
• Operational burden: 20+ inspections per flight, 6-month turnover

Lesson learned: Simplicity enabled rapid turnaround (SpaceX goal of 10-flight-per-engine)
```

**Nozzle Design (De Laval Nozzle):**

```
Supersonic Rocket Nozzle:

                 Chamber (subsonic)
                      │
                      ▼
              ┌────────────────┐
              │   Converging   │ P: 200 bar
              │   Section      │ V: Subsonic
              │                │ T: 3,600 K
              └────────┬───────┘
                       │
                  Throat (sonic)
                  ┌────────┐   P: ~50-100 bar
                  │ Mach 1 │   V: Sound speed (~1,200 m/s)
                  │ point  │   T: 3,300 K
                  └────┬───┘
                       │
              ┌────────▼───────┐
              │  Diverging     │ P: ~1 bar (or lower)
              │  Section       │ V: Hypersonic (3,000-5,000 m/s)
              │ (expansion)    │ T: 2,500 K (lower by expansion cooling)
              └────────┬───────┘
                       │
                   ▼ Exhaust

Design theory:
• Converging section: Accelerates flow to Mach 1 at throat
• Diverging section: Further expansion (counter-intuitive!) increases velocity above Mach 1
• Expansion through diverging section converts internal energy (temperature) to kinetic energy

Specific Impulse optimized by:
• Throat diameter (affects chamber pressure, expansion ratio)
• Diverging section angle (efficiency trade-off)
• Chamber volume (residence time for complete combustion)

Expansion ratio (A_exit / A_throat):
• Higher expansion ratio → higher exhaust velocity → higher I_sp
• Practical limit: ~40-100:1 for rocket engines
• Trade-off: Larger nozzle = more weight

Example (Space Shuttle SSME):
• Chamber pressure: 210 bar
• Expansion ratio: 77.5:1
• Throat diameter: 81 mm
• Exit diameter: 2.27 m
• I_sp: 450 seconds (vacuum)
```

**Regenerative Cooling:**

```
Problem: Combustion temperature (3,600 K) exceeds material melting point (~2,300 K).
Structure would fail without cooling.

Solution: Regenerative cooling

Concept:
Cold propellant (fuel or LOX) circulates through jacket around chamber & nozzle
Propellant absorbs heat → temperature rises from inlet to exit

Implementation (Merlin engine):
┌──────────────────────────────┐
│   Combustion Chamber          │ Hot side
│   Temp: 3,600 K              │ Inside: 3,600 K
│                              │ Outside: 400-600 K
│  ┌────────────────────────┐   │
│  │ Jacket (RP-1 coolant)  │   │ Cooling jacket
│  │ RP-1 in: 300 K         │   │
│  │ RP-1 out: 800 K        │   │ Outside: 300-400 K
│  └────────────────────────┘   │
└──────────────────────────────┘

Advantages:
+ Chamber wall stays below melting point (600-800 K safe margin)
+ Heats propellant slightly (minor I_sp benefit)
+ Passive system (no active cooling required)

Disadvantages:
– Complex cooling jacket manufacturing
– Pressure drop through jacket (turbopump must overcome)
– Potential for hot spots (maldistribution)

Effectiveness:
Regenerative cooling alone insufficient for high-performance engines.
Combination approach:
• Regenerative cooling (primary)
• Ablative coating (chamber walls)
• Copper or tungsten liners (high thermal conductivity)
• Porous cooling (propellant bleeds through chamber wall)
```

**Pump-Fed vs. Pressure-Fed vs. Gas-Generator Cycles:**

```
PUMP-FED CYCLE (most common: Saturn V, Falcon 9, SpaceX Raptor)
────────────────────────────────────────────
    Turbopump powered by hot gas (expander or gas-generator cycle)
    
    Advantages:
    + Very high chamber pressure (200+ bar, high performance)
    + High expansion ratio possible
    + Best I_sp
    
    Disadvantages:
    – Complex turbopump machinery
    – Reliability risk (many moving parts)
    – Weight penalty (turbopump + systems)
    – Cost (expensive manufacturing and qualification)

Cycle variants:
a) Expander cycle: Hot gas from nozzle cooling passages drives turbine
   (most efficient, but complex; used in SSME, not modern engines)
   
b) Gas-generator cycle: Small fraction of fuel/LOX burned separately
   to generate hot gas for turbine; exhaust dumped (simple, most common)
   
c) Tap-off cycle: Engine exhaust (after turbine) dumped (similar efficiency to gas-gen)


PRESSURE-FED CYCLE (simple, Goddard-era: early sounding rockets, some orbital vehicles)
──────────────────────────
    Inert gas (N₂ or He) pressurizes propellant tanks
    No turbopump
    
    Advantages:
    + Extremely simple
    + Reliable (few moving parts)
    + Long development heritage
    + Low cost
    
    Disadvantages:
    – Low chamber pressure (~20-40 bar, lower than pump-fed)
    – Lower I_sp (due to lower pressure)
    – Heavy inert gas needed (mass penalty)
    – Limited to small engines (scaling difficult)
    
    Modern use: None in large vehicles (too inefficient)
    Remaining use: Small satellite thrusters, suborbital rockets


GAS-GENERATOR CYCLE (most common modern implementation)
─────────────────────────────────
    Separate combustion of fuel/LOX drives turbine
    Turbine exhaust dumped (or used for engine cooling in some designs)
    
    Specifications (example: Merlin 1D):
    • Main chamber: LOX/RP-1, 250 bar
    • Gas generator: LOX/RP-1, 200 bar
    • Gas-gen feeds turbopump only
    • Main engine exhaust: Separate path to nozzle
    
    Advantages:
    + Decouples turbopump power from main engine
    + Simplifies design (turbopump optimized independently)
    + Good chamber pressure and I_sp
    + Single-use design possible (lower cost)
    
    Disadvantages:
    + Waste (turbine exhaust discarded = lost energy)
    + Less efficient than expander cycle
    + But simpler than expander (trade-off accepted)
```

### 4.2 Ascent Trajectory and Flight Phases

**Typical Ascent Profile (Falcon 9 to LEO, 185 km):**

```
PHASE 1: VERTICAL ASCENT (T+0 to T+10 seconds)
────────────────────────────
Altitude: 0 → 2 km
Velocity: 0 → 200 m/s
Trajectory: Nearly vertical (steering mostly vertical)

Control: 
• Thrust vector gimbal: Corrects attitude deviations
• Drag minimal (low velocity)
• Acoustic stress high (engines roaring)
• Shock waves in exhaust

Reason for vertical ascent:
• Maximize altitude gain early
• Minimize drag losses (density dropping rapidly)
• Leverage rocket's high initial thrust-to-weight


PHASE 2: GRAVITY TURN (T+10 to T+80 seconds)
──────────────────────
Altitude: 2 → 40 km
Velocity: 200 → 2,000 m/s
Trajectory: Gradually curving toward horizontal (30° → 60° pitch-over)

Mechanism: "Gravity turn"
• Pitch angle decreases automatically (no pilot control needed after initial program)
• Vehicle tilts as excess thrust accelerates structure
• Gravity provides required centripetal acceleration (toward circular orbit trajectory)
• No active control (passive; gravity naturally turns vehicle toward orbit)

Physics:
• Gravity pull: Always downward, 10 m/s² (decreasing slightly with altitude)
• Centrifugal effect (apparent, in rotating frame): Pulls outward
• Combination: Naturally curves trajectory toward tangential (horizontal)

Analogy: Ball on a string — string pulls toward center, ball moves tangentially

Timing: Critical window (~10 seconds) determines final orbit
• Too early gravity turn: Excess velocity at injection, higher orbit
• Too late gravity turn: Insufficient velocity, suborbital


PHASE 3: MAX-Q (Maximum Dynamic Pressure, T+60-80 seconds)
──────────────────
Altitude: ~15 km
Velocity: 500 m/s
Dynamic pressure: q = 0.5 × ρ × v²

Peak dynamic pressure: ~30-35 kPa (3-3.5× atmospheric pressure at sea level)

Significance:
• Maximum structural stress on vehicle (bending loads from air pressure)
• Leads to throttling events (one or more engines shut down to reduce thrust)
• Engines resume thrust after passing (pressure decreases with altitude)

Merlin throttling:
• Typical: 60-70% thrust during Max-Q
• Reduces force on structure
• Slightly extends burn time (acceptable)
• Burn time now: ~170 seconds (vs. 162 without throttling)

Recovery:
• Altitude passes 25 km
• Air density drops exponentially
• Dynamic pressure decreases
• Engines throttle back to full thrust

Stress analysis:
• Vehicle must withstand bending moment: F_lateral = q × A (dynamic pressure × area)
• Aluminum tanks designed for ~60 MPa bending stress
• Safety factor: 1.5× (so ultimate design is 90 MPa)
• Max-Q → 100 MPa would exceed safety margin → throttle

Engineering trade-off:
• Higher thrust (faster ascent) → higher Max-Q → stronger, heavier structure
• Lower thrust (gentler ascent) → lower Max-Q → lighter structure, longer burn time
• Optimum: Balance thrust vector and structural mass (Falcon 9 designed for this)


PHASE 4: FIRST STAGE BURNOUT & SEPARATION (T+162 seconds)
─────────────────────────
Altitude: 65 km
Velocity: 2,500 m/s (7.5 sound speed, still subsonic relative to background wind)
Trajectory: Apogee ~170 km (coast briefly upward)

MECO (Main Engine Cut-Off):
• Sensors detect LOX tank empty (most critical limit)
• Engine shutdown sequence initiated
• Fuel remaining: ~1% (RP-1 expended; little LOX left; no combustion possible)

Stage separation:
• Interstage explosive bolts detonate
• Spring pusher (mechanical actuator) shoves stages apart
• Relative velocity separation: 5-10 m/s

Objective: Prevent collision, prevent RP-1 slosh backward damaging first stage


PHASE 5: SECOND STAGE BURNOUT & ORBITAL INSERTION (T+162-530 seconds)
────────────────────────────
Duration: ~370 seconds (6 minutes)
Altitude: 65 km → 185 km (orbital altitude)
Velocity: 2,500 m/s → 7,800 m/s (orbital velocity)

Engines: Single Merlin vacuum engine (1,620 kN thrust vacuum)
Fuel: LOX/LH₂ (higher I_sp than first stage)
Specific impulse: 450 s (enabling final push to orbit with 1/9 the thrust of first stage)

Acceleration profile:
• Initial: ~2.5 m/s² (reduced thrust, lower mass after first stage separation)
• Final: ~5 m/s² (exhaust propellant, vehicle mass dropping rapidly)

Trajectory:
• Continues gravity turn (now at ~80-85° pitch, nearly horizontal)
• Orbits atmosphere (still feeling drag at 65+ km)
• Gains altitude while gaining velocity
• Reaches 185 km circular orbit (apogee matches perigee)

MECO (2nd stage, LOX depletion):
• T+530 seconds
• Altitude: 185 km
• Velocity: 7,800 m/s (orbital velocity)
• Trajectory: Circular, stable orbit

Result: Satellite now independent of rocket
Release/deployment:
• 1-2 minute coast (allow thermal settling)
• Explosive bolts sever payload retention
• Spring pusher ejects satellite
• Separation velocity: ~0.5 m/s (minimal disturbance)

Satellite now in orbit, carrying its own propellant for orbital adjustments


OPTIONAL: Booster Recovery (Falcon 9 reusable first stage)
──────────────────────
At T+0, first stage continues separate trajectory (after separation):

Reentry sequence:
• Apogee at ~65 km; begins falling (coasting downward)
• At 70 km: Grid fins deployed (aerodynamic braking begins)
• At 45 km: Engines reignited (5 engines, reduced thrust)
• Landing: Booster descends vertically, final hover adjustment
• Touchdown: Controlled landing @ drone ship or pad (similar to SpaceX achievements since 2015)

Complexity:
• Separate guidance computer on booster (independent mission)
• Fuel reserved: ~15% of total (not available for orbit)
• Two parallel missions: Satellite to orbit + booster recovery
• Trade-off: Lower payload capacity (~12,000 kg with landing vs. 22,800 kg expendable)

This adds enormous complexity but enables reusability economics
```

### 4.3 Launch Window & Orbital Mechanics

**Launch Window (Why Narrow?):**

```
Definition: Period during which launch achieves desired orbital characteristics

Constraints:

1. INCLINATION MATCHING (geographic constraint)
   • Desired orbit: 51.6° inclination (for ISS resupply missions)
   • Launch site: Kennedy Space Center, 28.5° N latitude
   • Vehicle latitude at launch: Must be ≥ launch site latitude
     
   Physics:
   • Rocket launched due east: Gains Earth's rotation velocity (465 m/s at equator)
   • Vehicle latitude increases with trajectory (not due east → northeast)
   • Minimum achievable inclination: ~28.5° (launch site latitude)
   • To reach 51.6°: Launch toward northeast; sacrifices some eastward velocity
   
   Launch window:
   • Morning launches (dawn): Sun over Atlantic (crew visible, media purposes)
   • 10-20 minute window (5-10 seconds of usable launch time)
   • Limited by sun angle, crew sleep cycles, ground operations

2. RENDEZVOUS WINDOW (operational constraint)
   • Target: International Space Station at specific location in orbit
   • ISS orbital period: 92 minutes
   • Rendezvous: Dragon capsule chases ISS for ~2 days
   • Launch must occur when phasing allows correct approach
   • Complex mathematics determines exact launch second
   • Window: ~5-10 minutes total width; ~2-3 minute practical window

3. ATMOSPHERIC CONDITIONS (safety constraint)
   • Wind speed: Max 10-15 m/s (depends on vehicle type)
   • Temperature: -40°C to +35°C (cryogenic issues)
   • Lightning: No launch within 30 km of storm activity
   • Visibility: Minimum 10 km (debris tracking, if needed)
   • Humidity: Very high humidity can cause ice formation (dangerous)
   
   Frequency:
   • Favorable windows: ~3 days per month at Kennedy Space Center
   • Weather cancellation rate: ~40% (Florida summer thunderstorms)
```
